A market for lemons in software

Saved 2026-06-13 Draft economics ai software

This is where I keep trying to map the paper onto software, and where it still feels half-formed. I think there is an interesting counterpoint to the original lemons frame: in software, ambiguous quality might increase supply rather than shrink it. Right now I am trying to hold six claims together:

  1. The average quality is likely lower.
  2. The cost of new is much lower.
  3. Entrant supply may increase because quality is ambiguous and shipping rough software is cheap.
  4. Buyer distrust likely increases.
  5. The opportunity cost of not buying is lower, because a buyer can now build a low-quality substitute themselves.
  6. The knowledge gained by months of ownership is still real.

If those six are true at once, then first-contact evaluation gets weaker and long-horizon signals get stronger. Distrust rises, and the fallback option (“we can build something ourselves”) gets cheaper, even if that fallback is mediocre. Next to Akerlof’s lines about the middleman attracting lemons and the lender surviving through intimate local knowledge, I wrote: “(1) friction (2) knowledge.” That is the bridge I keep trying to make into software.

The question I keep circling is whether guanxiGuanxi is trust plus practical social knowledge inside an ongoing relationship. It carries more weight where contract enforcement is weak, slow, or not trusted. becomes the practical filter for distinguishing good software from bad software when quality is hard to verify up front.

Numerous institutions arise to counteract the effects of quality uncertainty. One obvious institution is guarantees. Most consumer durables carry guarantees to ensure the buyer of some normal expected quality.

A second example of an institution which counteracts the effects of quality uncertainty is the brand-name good. Brand names not only indicate quality but also give the consumer a means of retaliation if the quality does not meet expectations.

Informal unwritten guarantees are preconditions for trade and production.

George A. Akerlof, The Market for "Lemons", pp. 499-500

Brand signals belong here too. A first-hand signal is whether the software itself is well-regarded. A second-hand signal is whether it is used by, or invested in by, people who are well-regarded. That feels very close to Akerlof’s brand-name point, just with modern software wrappers around it.

Does any of this matter if we are no longer choosing software, but our agents choose our stack for us? Is the only relationship of trust then the one that our agents (1) make good decisions; (2) for our benefit?So the sorting layer may shift toward trust, reputation, and repeated observation over time.
