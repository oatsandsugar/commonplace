Firms are built on friction

Agents may be changing what this means.

Saved 2026-03-09 Draft economics ai labor

Ronald Coase, writing in 1937I found this paper through @hypersoren's X post, which was about the cost of inference and the pricing power of frontier labs, not directly about the firm. The Coase application here is mine. Soren himself took a different Coase angle later, in Against The Coasean Singularity (2026-03-21): agents do not shrink the firm because context revelation becomes the new transaction cost, and firms vertically integrate to hoard it. His conclusion runs opposite to mine. My reading takes a narrower question: what Coase implies for firms that merely consume agents, before the context-scarcity equilibrium kicks in., asked a question most of economics skips past: why do firms exist at all? If markets allocate resources efficiently, why are there large pockets of planned, hierarchical activity sitting inside them?

His answer was cost, specifically the cost of using the market itself:

A firm will tend to expand until the costs of organising an extra transaction within the firm become equal to the costs of carrying out the same transaction by means of an exchange on the open market.

Ronald Coase, The Nature of the Firm (1937)

Restated: firms exist to minimize transaction costs. They grow larger whenever hiring is cheaper than transacting, and smaller whenever the reverse is true. The boundary of the firm is drawn exactly where those two costs cross.

The reason to keep this now is that agents change the cost of transacting. If an agent can negotiate, procure, and settle a contract for a cent, the transaction-cost argument for keeping a function in-house weakens. Coase's equilibrium shifts. The firm's boundary should move. Outward or inward is the interesting question.

A half-formed thought: Block's layoffs

In February 2026, BlockBlock is not alone. Companies that cut workforce citing AI directly: Salesforce (4,000 customer-service roles, Sept 2025; Benioff's “I need less heads” quote came on the Logan Bartlett Show); Klarna (~700 service reps, 2023–24, per their SEC F-1 prospectus; Siemiatkowski later conceded the AI had degraded service quality and began rehiring); Amazon (Jassy's June 2025 memo tied the shrink directly to AI; 14,000 corporate roles cut Oct 2025); Google (Pichai framed December-2024 management-layer cuts as freeing funds for the AI pivot, though delivered at an internal all-hands and reported only via press); Duolingo (von Ahn's “AI-first” memo, April 2025: “gradually stop using contractors to do work that AI can handle”). Counter-case: Microsoft ran ~15,000 layoffs through 2025 and Nadella's “enigma of success” memo explicitly rejected AI as the cause, even as GitHub Copilot was writing up to 30% of new code internally. cut about 40% of its workforce. Jack Dorsey's stated reason was not financial difficulty but the “growing capability of AI tools to perform a wider range of tasks.” The stock rose 24% on the news.

Dorsey's frame is internal: AI makes each remaining worker more productive, so fewer are needed. That is a labor-productivity story. But Coase suggests a reading that runs alongside it: if agents drop the cost of transacting in the market, the boundary of the firm has to contract. Work that used to be cheaper inside becomes cheaper outside.

Here is what I keep circling: these are two pressures, and they are not the same. One says “we got faster internally.” That is Block's story. The other says “the market got faster at serving us.” That is Coase's story, translated. A firm losing people to the first is celebrating productivity. A firm losing people to the second is failing to lower the price of its internal interactions as quickly as other market participants are lowering the price of doing business with it.

From the outside they look identical: smaller firm, share price up. Which story is actually true determines whether the remaining workforce is running toward a frontier or being overtaken by it. I do not know enough yet to tell them apart.

Source:
Coase, R. H. (1937). “The Nature of the Firm.” Economica, 4(16), 386–405.
