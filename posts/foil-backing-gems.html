A gem backed with foil

Saved 2026-06-10 etymology literature language

The word foil comes from the old practice of backing gems with foil to make them shine more brightly.

Wikipedia, Foil (narrative)

The etymology encodes the job description. A foil character isn’t there to be seen; they’re placed behind the protagonist to bounce light back through them. Jewelers literally did this — a thin sheet of metal behind a stone to make it glitter.Shakespeare uses the image directly, and knowingly. In Henry IV, Part 1, Prince Hal promises that his reformation will impress precisely because of the dissolute youth behind it:

And like bright metal on a sullen ground,
My reformation, glittering o’er my fault,
Shall show more goodly and attract more eyes
Than that which hath no foil to set it off.

Shakespeare, Henry IV, Part 1, 1.2

And in Hamlet he puns it twice over: “I’ll be your foil, Laertes” — the literary foil and the fencing foil, in a fencing scene, between two characters who are foils to each other.
