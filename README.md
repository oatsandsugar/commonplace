# commonplace

A place to think about a pattern I keep falling into: small, personal, durable catalogues of things I care about, with annotations explaining why.

## What a commonplace book is

A notebook — the form goes back to antiquity but peaked in the Renaissance and early-modern period — where people copied quotations, observations, recipes, and ideas they wanted to keep, organized by theme rather than by date.

It sits between three things it isn't:
- **A diary** is chronological and about your day.
- **A scrapbook** is collected but rarely annotated.
- **A library catalogue** is organized but impersonal.

A commonplace book is organized *and* annotated *and* personal. You wrote down the Ovid passage and also why it mattered to you, and then you indexed it so you could find it later.

Erasmus wrote a manual on how to keep one. Milton, Francis Bacon, John Locke, and Thomas Jefferson all kept them. Most never had an audience beyond their owner; the ones that survived ended up read for centuries.

## Principles from the people who wrote about this

Different emphases, family resemblance.

- **Erasmus, *De Copia* (1512)** — the founding text. Start with headings (virtue, prudence, friendship, etc.) and copy passages under each. Purpose: rhetorical fluency. The key word is *copia*, abundance. You're not indexing a domain; you're building verbal muscle.
- **Francis Bacon** — the categories matter more than the entries. The headings you choose shape what you notice. Few, sharp categories beat many fuzzy ones. A badly organized commonplace is worse than nothing, because you can't find what you put in.
- **John Locke, *A New Method of Making Common-Place-Books* (1706)** — almost entirely about retrieval. Famously proposed an indexing grid (first letter + first vowel of the keyword → a cell that lists page numbers). Without findability, the book dies.
- **Milton** — three-part moral architecture (ethics / household / politics) with subtopics. Cross-referenced aggressively; the same passage lived under multiple headings. Wrote passages out in full rather than summarizing.
- **Jefferson** — kept two (legal and literary). Interpolated his own commentary with the copied passages. Used them as working tools; the legal one shaped his legal arguments, the literary one shaped his writing.

Cross-cutting, distilled:

1. **Topic, not chronology.** That's what separates a commonplace from a diary.
2. **Architecture is upfront and stable; contents are mutable.** Headings are fixed, entries accumulate under them.
3. **Retrieval is the whole problem.** Without an index it's a graveyard.
4. **Annotate, don't just copy.** A commonplace without commentary is a scrapbook.
5. **Keep for yourself first.** None of them published these. The ones that survived survived because they were honest, not performed.
6. **Write things in full.** Don't pre-compress meaning you might want back.
7. **Read in order to write.** The book is a tool, not an end.
8. **Abundance over completeness.** You're not indexing a domain; you're building fluency via what you personally encounter.

## Prior art on the web

- **[gwern.net](https://gwern.net)** — Gwern Branwen. Probably the clearest example of a commonplace-shaped site on the open web: organized by topic, obsessively annotated, heavily cross-linked, designed for decades of lifespan. Explicitly inspired by the tradition.
- **[maggieappleton.com](https://maggieappleton.com)** — Maggie Appleton. A digital garden, and she's written the best essays I've found on the modern commonplace / digital-garden revival (e.g. *A Brief History & Ethos of the Digital Garden*). Read her before anyone else.

## Why I'm opening this directory

readingstori.es (books-for-indigo) is one. It's a personal catalogue of picture books, organized by year / author / award / country / star, annotated with why each book earned its place. Kept primarily so I can re-find and re-think. Durable enough that someone else can read it.

I suspect a few of my other projects are the same shape without me having named it. Worth making the pattern explicit, because once I notice it I'll probably use it better.

## Authorial decisions

A blog-with-tags pattern is a good chassis for a commonplace. The things it lets you skip — that a commonplace makes you actually decide — are authorial, not technical. Roughly in order of how often they'll bite you:

1. **Tag discipline: tag free-form, canonicalize later.** Per Maggie Appleton's garden practice. Tag each entry with 2–4 words as you write, ad-hoc, without consulting a master list. At ~20 entries, do a *tag audit*: list everything used, cluster the synonyms, pick the canonical set, collapse variants. Re-audit at 50 and 100. This avoids predicting the topology of work you haven't written, and keeps Bacon's warning from cutting the wrong way — a pre-committed list pulls you toward entries that fit it.
2. **Editing policy.** Blogs default to "post and forget." Commonplaces default to "update the canonical entry as I learn more." Decide upfront: does an entry grow forever (with dated revision marks), or do you post a new one each time the thought develops? Both work; pick one.
3. **Cross-referencing in prose, not just tags.** Tags give weak clustering. Real connections come from writing "see also [X]" into the body, because that forces you to name *how* two entries are connected. Locke and Milton both did this constantly.
4. **Source capture.** Record sources well enough that future-you can re-find the original: not "a Calvino essay" but "Calvino, *Why Read the Classics*, essay 3, p. 17." Blogs are lazy about this; commonplaces aren't.
5. **Keep for yourself first.** A public blog's default gravity pulls toward performance. The commonplace tradition fought this by being private. Stay alert to the drift.
6. **Topic-page design.** Default tag pages are "list of posts, newest first." That's ugly for a commonplace. Each topic page probably wants a short canonical intro ("what I think about this right now"), entries listed in curated order below (not strict chronology), and a "see also" section linking sibling topics.
7. **Finished vs in-progress.** Gardens use *seedling / budding / evergreen* markers. You don't need that vocabulary, but you need a way to signal "this is half-baked, don't cite me yet." Otherwise you'll either refuse to publish anything messy, or publish messy things without flagging them.
8. **Heterogeneity tolerance.** Decide what counts as a valid entry. A book review, a quoted passage, a conversation overheard, a recipe, a counter-argument to something you read — the Renaissance commonplaces allowed all of it. Narrowing to one genre turns it into a specialist blog. That's fine, but it's a choice.
9. **Retrieval, beyond tags.** At least two ways to find an old entry: full-text search, a hand-curated table of contents, or a "latest" feed separate from "canonical." Tags alone fail when you can't remember the tag.

The thing that ties all nine: you're building for a reader who is primarily yourself, ten years from now. Everything above is a question of "will future-me be able to use this?"
